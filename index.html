Forecasting Point Trajectories in 3D with Language Instruction

Abstract

Motion forecasting is central to visual intelligence: agents must anticipate how objects will move in order to plan actions, reason about physical interactions, and synthesize realistic futures. We argue that 3D points in world coordinates provide a general motion representation that is class-agnostic, view-stable, compact, and directly useful for downstream tasks. We formalize the task of goal-conditioned 3D point motion forecasting: given a short visual history, a set of 3D query points on an object, and a language description of the intended goal, the model predicts the future 3D trajectory of each point. We introduce a full stack to study this task at scale: (1) MolmoMotion-1M, a large corpus of action-described, object-grounded 3D point trajectories annotated from 1.16M unconstrained videos; (2) PointMotionBench, a human-verified benchmark spanning 111 object categories and 61 motion types; and (3) MolmoMotion, a general motion forecasting model that supports both autoregressive coordinate prediction and flow-matching trajectory generation. MolmoMotion accurately predicts diverse motion patterns from language instructions and significantly outperforms existing motion prediction baselines on PointMotionBench. We further show that the learned 3D motion prior transfers well to downstream applications: it improves training efficiency and generalization for robot manipulation, and its predicted trajectories provide effective motion guidance for generative video models.

3D Motion Forecasting

MolmoMotion forecasts diverse 3D motion types across objects, scenes, and action descriptions. The predicted tracks follow the language instruction and remain close to ground truth across both manipulation and outdoor dynamic scenes.

On PointMotionBench, MolmoMotion outperforms prior methods by a large margin, beating pixel-space video generators, parametric 3D methods, and constant-velocity extrapolation. The autoregressive variant achieves the strongest overall performance under deterministic trajectory metrics. Notably, simple non-parametric baselines are competitive with several learned baselines, suggesting that visually plausible RGB futures do not necessarily recover accurate metric point motion.

Downstream: Robotics Planning

A useful motion prior should transfer across embodiments: a cup lifted by a human hand and by a robot gripper involve very different actions, but the object can follow a similar 3D path. After fine-tuning on DROID, MolmoMotion predicts coherent object trajectories in real robot videos across different objects, viewpoints, scenes, and manipulation tasks.

The learned prior improves sample efficiency, closed-loop success, and generalization to unseen objects and scenes. On the MolmoSpaces pick-and-place task, a policy initialized from MolmoMotion reached 76.3% success versus 56.0% for a Molmo 2 baseline, and learned faster (51% at 10K steps vs 19%). On real robots, MolmoMotion fine-tuned on DROID reached the baseline's 12K-step error in roughly 2K steps.

Downstream: Video Generation

MolmoMotion's predicted 3D trajectories provide accurate motion guidance for video generation. We supply explicit future object trajectories as a motion-control signal for image-to-video models. The 3D tracks help generated videos follow the requested action more precisely, especially for fine-grained object interactions where text alone is ambiguous.

"A flamingo dips its beak into the water while walking to the right"

"Take the round light brown plate from the table"

When its predicted trajectories condition a lightweight 3D-track-conditioned image-to-video model, MolmoMotion improves video quality over the base model across all five motion-related metrics, and outperforms a much larger image-to-video model on four of the five.

The MolmoMotion Model

MolmoMotion uses the pretrained 4B Molmo 2 as its vision-language backbone, connecting language instructions to objects and points in the image. Given a short video history, an action description, and a set of query points with their initial 3D positions, the model builds a shared understanding of the referred object, the query points, and the described motion, then predicts the future 3D trajectory of each point. We train two complementary variants:

  • The autoregressive variant (MolmoMotion-AR) predicts future coordinates step by step as structured text, in temporal order. Conditioning each new coordinate on the trajectory generated so far encourages smooth rollouts and gives the strongest accuracy when the future path is well-defined.
  • The flow-matching variant (MolmoMotion-FM) predicts trajectories in continuous 3D space by transforming noise into motion, making it better suited to representing uncertainty when an instruction admits multiple plausible futures.

MolmoMotion-1M and PointMotionBench

To train MolmoMotion, we need data that does not yet exist: large-scale videos with 3D point trajectories grounded to specific objects and paired with action descriptions. Existing 3D-track datasets are small and domain-limited, and internet videos provide scale and diversity but lack 3D annotations. We therefore built an automatic pipeline that extracts object-grounded 3D trajectories in metric world coordinates from unconstrained video. It yields MolmoMotion-1M, the largest corpus of action-described, object-grounded 3D point trajectories assembled to date, spanning 736 motion types and 5,692 distinct objects from 1.16M video clips.

To evaluate motion forecasting, we built PointMotionBench, a human-verified benchmark of held-out 3D trajectories covering 2,720 clips across 111 object categories and 61 motion types, including indoor manipulation, egocentric hand-object interaction, and outdoor dynamic scenes. For each clip, models receive the current observation, object query points, and an action description, and are evaluated on how accurately their predicted 3D point trajectories match the object's actual future motion, giving a direct quantitative test of 3D motion forecasting.

Limitations

MolmoMotion requires multiple forward passes to predict dense point tracks on an object, since pretraining uses only 8 query points per example due to the Molmo 2 context-length limit. This sparse-point setting is not sufficient to densely represent object geometry, limiting the model's understanding of fine-grained structure and complex deformable motion. In addition, more downstream evaluations, such as closed-loop real-world robot experiments, are needed to fully establish the effectiveness of this motion-pretraining task.
